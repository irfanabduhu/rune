# Voice Calibration Process

Before writing any fiction, perform this analysis for the named author. Do not skip steps. Do not rely on generic impressions. The goal is to internalize the author's specific, nameable techniques so deeply that the resulting fiction could plausibly belong in their body of work.

---

## The Ten Dimensions of Voice

Analyze the author across each dimension. For each, identify SPECIFIC, NAMEABLE techniques — not vague characterizations like "writes beautifully" or "uses short sentences."

### 1. Sentence Architecture

- **Length distribution**: What is the typical sentence length? Does the author favor uniformity or dramatic variation? Hemingway averages 10–15 words with occasional 40-word compound sentences chained by "and." Faulkner averages 35–50 words with sentences that can run to 200+. Carver keeps to 8–12 words almost religiously.
- **Complexity**: Paratactic (coordinate clauses joined by "and") vs. hypotactic (nested subordination). Hemingway is paratactic. Henry James is hypotactic. Most authors mix both — identify the ratio.
- **Fragments**: Does the author use sentence fragments? How? Cormac McCarthy uses fragments as standalone paragraphs for emphasis. Carver uses fragments in dialogue and interior thought. Didion uses fragments for rhythm.
- **Conjunction patterns**: Polysyndeton (repeated "and") is Hemingway's signature. Asyndeton (omitted conjunctions) is McCarthy's. Woolf chains clauses with semicolons and em-dashes.
- **Characteristic constructions**: Identify 2–3 sentence structures this author returns to repeatedly. Examples:
  - Woolf: long sentences with em-dash parentheticals that shift perspective mid-sentence
  - David Foster Wallace: sentences with footnote-like parenthetical asides nested 2–3 levels deep
  - García Márquez: sentences that begin in the present and sweep backward through decades in a single clause
  - Kafka: sentences that begin with matter-of-fact declaratives and end with quietly impossible conclusions

### 2. Paragraph Rhythm

- **Length**: Short, punchy paragraphs (Carver, McCarthy) vs. long dense blocks (Faulkner, Proust) vs. varied (most authors). What is the typical range?
- **Opening patterns**: Does the author open paragraphs with action, observation, dialogue, or interiority? Hemingway opens with action or concrete detail. Woolf opens with perception or thought. Austen opens with social observation.
- **Closing patterns**: Do paragraphs close with resolution, a new question, an image, or trailing off? Chekhov closes paragraphs with images. Hemingway closes with understated action.
- **Single-sentence paragraphs**: How often and for what effect? McCarthy uses them constantly for stark emphasis. Hemingway uses them for dialogue and for moments of suppressed emotion.

### 3. Diction

- **Register**: Formal (James, Nabokov), colloquial (Twain, Salinger), mixed (Austen's free indirect discourse blends formal narration with characters' idiom), clinical (Didion), lyrical (Morrison), sparse (Carver).
- **Vocabulary range**: Narrow and precise (Hemingway: common words used with exactitude) vs. vast and ornate (Nabokov: rare words deployed for texture and phonetic pleasure) vs. technical (DeLillo: corporate and systems language infiltrating prose).
- **Recurring words**: Every author has verbal tics. Identify them.
  - Hemingway: "fine," "good," "nice" (used with ironic understatement); "and" beginning sentences
  - McCarthy: "and" beginning sentences; archaic terms ("suzerain," "sere")
  - Woolf: "one" as impersonal pronoun; "for" beginning explanatory clauses
  - Kafka: "evidently," "apparently," "it seemed" (epistemic hedging)
- **Forbidden words**: What words would this author NEVER use? Hemingway would never write "lugubrious" or "phantasmagoric." Carver would never write "resplendent." McCarthy would never write "basically" or "actually."

### 4. Dialogue Style

- **Tag conventions**: "Said" only (Hemingway, McCarthy) vs. varied tags ("whispered," "exclaimed" — Dickens, popular fiction) vs. tagless (some McCarthy passages drop all attribution).
- **Tag position**: Before ("He said, '...'"), after ("'...' he said"), or mid-sentence ("'I think,' he said, 'we should go'")? Each author has a pattern.
- **Dialogue-to-narration ratio**: Estimate the percentage. Hemingway: 60–70% dialogue in some stories. Woolf: 10–20%. Borges: nearly 0%.
- **How characters speak**: Do all characters sound alike (Hemingway — everyone speaks in the same clipped style) or distinctly different (Dickens — each character has a verbal fingerprint)? Is dialogue realistic or stylized?
- **Subtext**: How much goes unsaid in dialogue? Hemingway: almost everything important is beneath the surface. Pinter: characters speak to avoid saying what they mean. Austen: what characters don't say reveals more than what they do.
- **Punctuation**: Does the author use quotation marks? McCarthy famously does not. Does the author use dashes for interrupted speech? Austen uses them frequently.

### 5. Narrative Perspective

- **Preferred POV**: First person (Fitzgerald, Ishiguro), third limited (James, Munro), third omniscient (Tolstoy, Eliot), second person (rare — Italo Calvino, some Lorrie Moore). Some authors shift — Faulkner rotates narrators.
- **Narrative distance**: Close third (Woolf — we are inside the character's stream of consciousness) vs. distant third (Tolstoy — we see the character from a knowing, almost divine vantage).
- **Free indirect discourse**: Does the author blend narrator voice and character thought without attribution? Austen does this constantly ("It is a truth universally acknowledged" — is that the narrator or society speaking?). Flaubert pioneered it. Woolf dissolves the boundary entirely.
- **Reliability**: Is the narrator always trustworthy? Ishiguro's narrators suppress and rationalize. Nabokov's narrators are brilliant liars.

### 6. Thematic Obsessions

- **Core questions**: What does this author always return to, regardless of plot?
  - Hemingway: how to live with dignity under pressure; the relationship between physical courage and moral integrity
  - Kafka: the individual's powerlessness before systems; the body as site of transformation and punishment
  - Woolf: the texture of consciousness; how time erodes and preserves; the gap between public selves and inner life
  - Le Guin: what social structures we accept as natural; the ethics of power; how language shapes thought
  - Morrison: the weight of history on the body; community as both refuge and prison; the cost of self-invention
- **Recurring situations**: What scenarios does this author return to?
  - Hemingway: men in war, fishing, bullfighting — testing grounds for character
  - Austen: women navigating the marriage market under economic constraint
  - Borges: scholars discovering impossible texts, infinite libraries, labyrinths
  - Chekhov: people failing to communicate what matters most in ordinary settings
- **Moral stance**: Does the author judge characters openly (Dickens), sympathize without judgment (Chekhov), maintain ironic distance (Austen), or refuse moral framing entirely (McCarthy)?

### 7. What Is Left Unsaid

- **Subtext density**: How much does the author leave to implication? Hemingway's "iceberg theory" — seven-eighths of the story's meaning lives beneath the surface. Carver leaves out the epiphany the reader expects. Chekhov trusts the reader to feel what characters cannot articulate.
- **Elision**: What does the author characteristically skip or suppress?
  - McCarthy: punctuation itself (apostrophes, quotation marks)
  - Carver: emotional processing — characters feel things but don't name them
  - Hemingway: the traumatic event — we see before and after but never the moment itself
  - Ishiguro: the narrator's own culpability — it surfaces only through cracks in the telling
- **Reader trust**: How much does the author expect the reader to infer? High-trust authors (Hemingway, Chekhov, Carver) leave enormous gaps. Low-trust authors (Dickens, most genre fiction) fill in every implication. Where does this author fall?

### 8. Opening and Closing Strategies

- **First lines**: Mentally recall the author's actual first lines to identify patterns.
  - Kafka: matter-of-fact statements of impossibility ("One morning, when Gregor Samsa woke from troubled dreams, he found himself transformed in his bed into a horrible vermin.")
  - Austen: ironic general observations ("It is a truth universally acknowledged...")
  - García Márquez: temporal leaps ("Many years later, as he faced the firing squad, Colonel Aureliano Buendía was to remember that distant afternoon when his father took him to discover ice.")
  - Tolstoy: sweeping philosophical declarations ("Happy families are all alike...")
  - Camus: flat, affectless statements ("Mother died today. Or maybe yesterday; I can't be sure.")
- **Last lines / endings**: How does the author typically end?
  - Chekhov: ambiguous — life continues, nothing is resolved, but something has shifted
  - O'Connor: violent grace — a moment of devastating revelation, often physical
  - Joyce: epiphanic — the character (and reader) glimpses something true
  - Borges: a final inversion that reframes everything preceding it
  - Carver: understated — a small gesture or observation that carries enormous weight
  - Hemingway: suppressed emotion — the final sentence says less than it means

### 9. Sensory and Spatial Detail

- **Dominant senses**: What does this author notice?
  - Hemingway: visual and tactile — the exact color of water, the feel of a trout on the line
  - Proust: taste and smell — the madeleine, the hawthorn
  - Woolf: light and movement — how light falls on surfaces, how bodies move through rooms
  - Morrison: the body's heat, weight, and texture — skin, hair, the feel of dirt
  - McCarthy: landscape as geological and mythic — vast spaces described with biblical attention
- **Spatial description**: Precise and geographic (Hemingway — you could draw a map), impressionistic (Woolf — spaces are felt, not measured), absent (Borges — settings are conceptual, not physical), oppressive (Kafka — spaces shrink and trap).
- **The body**: How are bodies described?
  - Morrison: sensuously, with attention to skin, hands, the physical traces of history
  - Kafka: as objects of transformation, degradation, and institutional punishment
  - McCarthy: as things that break, bleed, and endure
  - Hemingway: through what they do, not how they look — bodies in action

### 10. Temporal Handling

- **Tense**: Past tense (most realist fiction), present tense (some contemporary writers — second-person Lorrie Moore, some Carver), mixed or shifting.
- **Time movement**: Linear (most realists), nonlinear (Faulkner — time fragments and recurs; Woolf — the present dilates while years compress), circular (García Márquez — time repeats across generations; Borges — time forks and loops).
- **Scene vs. summary**: What does the author dramatize in full scene vs. compress into narrative summary?
  - Hemingway: dramatizes almost everything — long scene, minimal summary
  - Woolf: compresses external events ("Time Passes" covers years in pages) and expands internal moments (a dinner party fills a hundred pages)
  - García Márquez: leaps across decades in a sentence, then slows to real-time for a pivotal moment
  - Borges: summarizes what a novelist would dramatize — Borges reviews fictional books rather than writing them
- **Flashback conventions**: Does the author use explicit flashbacks (Fitzgerald), embedded memories that blur into the present (Woolf, Faulkner), or a strict chronological line (Hemingway, mostly)?

---

## Calibration Output

After analyzing all ten dimensions, produce a concise internal voice profile: one paragraph per dimension, with SPECIFIC techniques and examples identified. This profile guides all subsequent writing. Refer back to it at every decision point during drafting.

The profile should be concrete enough that another writer could read it and produce passable mimicry from the profile alone.

---

## The Imitation Test

After calibrating, mentally compose a single opening sentence for the requested story. Ask yourself:

> If I covered the author attribution, could a well-read person — seeing only this sentence — guess which author this is mimicking?

If not, the voice is too generic. Recalibrate. Identify what is missing: is it the sentence structure? The diction? The relationship between narrator and subject? Adjust until the sentence passes the test.

Only then proceed to writing.

---

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Surface mimicry**: Writing short sentences and calling it Hemingway. Short sentences are necessary but not sufficient — you also need the suppressed emotion, the concrete detail, the "and" chains, the dialogue-heavy scenes, and the iceberg subtext.
2. **Caricature**: Exaggerating one trait until it becomes parody. Faulkner's long sentences are not random run-ons — they have internal architecture. McCarthy's sparse style is not merely missing punctuation — it has rhythmic intention.
3. **Anachronism**: Using vocabulary or references that postdate the author's era. A story "in the style of Austen" should not contain words that entered English after 1817.
4. **Plot over voice**: Focusing on the kind of story the author would write rather than HOW they would write it. Voice is sentence-level. Plot is necessary but secondary.
5. **Forgetting the author's silences**: What an author does NOT do is as distinctive as what they do. Hemingway's refusal to name emotions. Chekhov's refusal to resolve. Borges' refusal to dramatize. These absences ARE the style.
